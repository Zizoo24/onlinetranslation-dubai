NOTARY PUBLIC ATTESTATION GUIDE

Understanding MOA Translation Requirements in the UAE

The Memorandum of Association is not a standard commercial contract. It is a foundational legal instrument that establishes your company's existence, defines its purpose, and allocates rights and obligations among shareholders. When translating this document, precision is paramount because the Arabic version becomes the operative text for most UAE government interactions.

In the UAE, Arabic is the official language for government filings and court proceedings. This means that when you submit your MOA to the Department of Economic Development for license renewal, to a bank for corporate account opening, or to a court in the event of a shareholder dispute, the Arabic translation is what officials will read and rely upon. If there is any discrepancy between the English and Arabic versions, the Arabic text generally prevails. This reality elevates the importance of translation accuracy far beyond simple language conversion.

Consider a common scenario: your MOA states that a shareholder holds "49% of the company's shares." In Arabic, there are specific legal terms for share ownership, and the choice of words can affect how this ownership is interpreted. A translator unfamiliar with UAE corporate conventions might use phrasing that sounds correct but introduces ambiguity about voting rights, profit distribution, or transfer restrictions. The result can be delays at DED, questions from bank compliance officers, or disputes that require legal intervention to resolve.

Similarly, the "objects" or business activities clause in your MOA defines what your company is legally permitted to do. Translating these activity descriptions requires knowledge of how DED categorizes business licenses. If the translation uses generic terms instead of the specific activity descriptions that DED recognizes, you may face difficulties adding activities to your license or expanding into new business lines.

For MOAs executed abroad, additional attestation steps are required before translation. The typical chain includes notarization in the country of origin, authentication by the foreign ministry or equivalent, attestation by the UAE Embassy in that country, and finally MOFA attestation upon arrival in the UAE. We guide clients through this attestation sequence and can advise on the specific requirements based on the country of origin and intended use of the translated document.

MODULE A

Compliance Checklist: Mainland vs. Freezone MOA

Before translating your Memorandum of Association, it is essential to understand the regulatory framework your company operates within. Mainland and freezone MOAs have different structures, required articles, and terminology conventions that affect how we approach the translation.

Mainland MOA Structure and Required Articles

Mainland companies registered with the Department of Economic Development (DED) follow the UAE Commercial Companies Law (Federal Decree-Law No. 32 of 2021 and its amendments). A compliant mainland MOA typically includes the following elements that require careful translation:

Company Name and Form

The legal name must be translated consistently throughout the document, including any trade name variations. The company form (LLC, Sole Establishment, Civil Company, etc.) must use the corresponding Arabic legal term recognized by DED.

Capital Structure and Share Allocation

The total share capital, whether AED 1 or AED 100 million, must be stated precisely. Partner contributions — whether cash, in-kind, or a combination — are translated with the specific values and percentages. For LLCs, the minimum percentage holdings and any restrictions on share transfer must be accurately rendered.

Business Activities (Objects Clause)

DED maintains a specific classification system for business activities. The translation must align with DED's recognized activity descriptions so that license applications, renewals, and activity additions proceed without objection.

Management and Authority

Provisions about who manages the company, signatory authority for contracts and bank transactions, and the appointment and removal of managers must be translated to reflect the intended authority structure. Ambiguity here can create practical problems when dealing with banks or signing commercial agreements.

Profit and Loss Distribution

How profits are divided among partners — whether proportional to shareholding or on a different basis — must be clearly stated. This is particularly important for tax and accounting purposes under the new UAE corporate tax regime.

Dispute Resolution and Governing Law

Clauses specifying how disputes are resolved (UAE courts, arbitration, mediation) and which law governs the MOA must be translated using established legal terminology that UAE courts recognize.

Freezone MOA Differences by Authority

Each free zone authority has its own regulations, templates, and terminology preferences. Understanding these differences is essential for accurate translation:

DMCC (Dubai Multi Commodities Centre)

DMCC MOAs often use specific terminology related to commodities trading, gold trading, and consultancy services. The authority has its own prescribed format, and translations must align with their portal requirements for company registration and amendments.

JAFZA (Jebel Ali Free Zone)

JAFZA companies may have MOAs that reference warehousing, logistics, and manufacturing activities. The translation must use terminology that JAFZA's registration team recognizes when processing license applications or renewals.

DIFC (Dubai International Financial Centre)

DIFC operates under common law principles derived from English law. MOAs for DIFC entities often include provisions about directors, company secretaries, and registered agents that differ from mainland or other freezone structures. The translation must preserve these common law concepts while rendering them in Arabic for any required filings.

ADGM (Abu Dhabi Global Market)

Similar to DIFC, ADGM follows a common law framework. MOAs may include provisions about nominee directors, corporate governance standards, and regulated activities that require specialized translation expertise.

Other Free Zones

Dubai Silicon Oasis, Dubai Media City, Dubai Internet City, RAK Free Zone, and numerous other authorities each have their own conventions. We research the specific requirements of your free zone before translating to ensure compliance.

Share Structure Translation: Key Considerations

Share structure provisions require particular attention because they define ownership rights and financial interests:

Percentage Holdings

When your MOA states that Partner A holds 51% and Partner B holds 49%, we ensure these percentages appear correctly in Arabic with the proper terminology for shareholding. We verify that the translated percentages sum correctly and match any schedules or annexes to the MOA.

Share Capital and Par Value

Total authorized capital, issued capital, and paid-up capital are translated with their exact figures. For companies with shares having a par value, we translate this correctly to avoid confusion about the nominal value of each share.

Capital Increases and Decreases

Provisions governing how the company can increase or decrease its capital, whether new shares can be issued, and any pre-emption rights for existing shareholders must be clearly translated so these mechanisms work as intended.

Share Transfer Restrictions

Many MOAs include restrictions on transferring shares — rights of first refusal, board approval requirements, or lock-up periods. These clauses affect how shares can be sold or transferred and must be accurately reflected in the Arabic version.

Classes of Shares

If your company has multiple share classes (ordinary shares, preference shares, management shares), each class and its associated rights must be clearly distinguished in translation.

Step-by-Step Process: Handling Complex Corporate Documents

MOA translation involves more than converting text from one language to another. We follow a structured process designed to catch potential issues early and deliver translations that work the first time they are presented to authorities.

Step 1: Document Receipt and Structure Analysis

When you send us your MOA, we begin with a comprehensive review. We identify the company type (mainland LLC, freezone FZE, DIFC Ltd, etc.), the number of shareholders, the capital structure, and the business activities. We check for completeness — are all pages present, are signatures and stamps visible, are any schedules or annexes included?

We also note where and when the MOA was executed. If it was signed abroad, we assess whether the attestation chain appears complete for use in the UAE. For MOAs that have been amended multiple times, we verify that we have the current consolidated version or all amendments in sequence.

If we identify potential issues — missing attestations, unclear scans, inconsistencies between different sections, or provisions that may not translate cleanly — we raise these before proceeding. Addressing problems at this stage prevents delays and additional costs later.

Step 2: Jurisdiction-Specific Terminology Mapping

Different UAE authorities use different terminology. Before translation begins, we map the key terms in your MOA to the Arabic equivalents that the relevant authority expects:

  • Company Names: Transliterating and translating company names consistently, including any trade name variations.
  • Legal Terms: Using the Arabic corporate law terms that DED, DMCC, or other authorities recognize for concepts like "limited liability," "share capital," "manager," and "partner."
  • Business Activities: Matching activity descriptions to the classification systems used by the relevant licensing authority.
  • Governance Terms: Translating provisions about board meetings, shareholder resolutions, and management authority using established legal conventions.

This mapping exercise ensures consistency throughout the translation and reduces the risk of rejection or questions from receiving authorities.

Step 3: Translation by MOJ-Licensed Legal Translator

The translation itself is executed by an MOJ-licensed legal translator with experience in corporate documents. We pay particular attention to:

  • Shareholder Details: Names, nationalities, passport numbers, and shareholding percentages must be translated exactly as they appear in the source document.
  • Capital Provisions: Figures are verified and translated with the correct Arabic numerals and currency references.
  • Governance Articles: Provisions about management, decision-making, and signatory authority are translated to preserve the intended power structure.
  • Standard Clauses: Boilerplate provisions about amendment procedures, dissolution, and dispute resolution are rendered using standard legal Arabic that courts and authorities recognize.
Step 4: Draft Review and Quality Assurance

Before final certification, we send you a draft translation for review. This is your opportunity to verify that:

  • Partner names are spelled correctly and match their identification documents
  • Shareholding percentages and capital figures are accurate
  • Business activities are correctly described
  • Any specific provisions important to your situation are properly translated

If you work with a corporate lawyer or PRO, this is a good time to have them review the Arabic text. We welcome feedback at this stage and can make corrections at no additional charge before final stamping.

Step 5: Certification, Stamping, and Delivery

Once you approve the draft, we proceed to final certification. The MOJ-licensed translator stamps each page with their official seal, signs the document, and includes their license details. You receive:

  • Digital copies (PDF) of the stamped translation for immediate use in email submissions or portal uploads
  • Physical hard copies with original stamps for in-person submissions to banks, DED, or other authorities
  • Delivery to your office, home, or a designated third party (bank, PRO, lawyer) as requested
MODULE C

After-Care Guide: What Comes After MOA Translation

A translated MOA is a tool, not an end in itself. The real value is realized when you use the translation to accomplish your business objectives — opening bank accounts, obtaining licenses, processing visa quotas, or demonstrating ownership to investors. This guide covers the practical next steps.

Corporate Bank Account Opening

Most UAE banks require a translated MOA as part of their corporate account opening documentation. Here is what to expect:

Documentation Package: Banks typically request the translated MOA along with the trade license, shareholder passports and visas, proof of address, and board resolution authorizing the account opening. We can translate the full package to ensure consistency across all documents.

Compliance Review: Bank compliance teams review the MOA to understand the ownership structure, identify beneficial owners, and verify that the company's activities align with the bank's risk appetite. A clearly translated MOA speeds up this review process.

Signatory Authority: Banks want to confirm who has authority to operate the account. If your MOA specifies signatory rights (sole signatory, joint signatories, transaction limits), the translation must clearly reflect these provisions.

Timeline Considerations: Bank account opening can take anywhere from a few days to several weeks depending on the bank and the complexity of your corporate structure. Having your translated MOA ready before you apply helps avoid delays.

DED Licensing and Activity Amendments

For mainland companies, DED requires translated corporate documents for various licensing activities:

License Renewals: While routine renewals may not require a fresh translation, any changes to shareholders, capital, or activities since the last renewal typically do.

Activity Additions: If you want to add new business activities to your license, DED may request an MOA translation to verify that the proposed activities fall within your company's permitted objects.

Branch Registration: Opening a branch of an existing company requires translated incorporation documents, including the MOA, to be filed with DED.

Partner Changes: When partners enter or exit the company, the amended MOA and share transfer documents need to be translated and filed with DED.

Visa Quota Applications

Your company's visa quota — the number of employment visas you can sponsor — is tied to your license and office space. Immigration authorities may request translated corporate documents when:

Initial Quota Establishment: New companies applying for their first visa quota typically need to submit translated MOA and license documents.

Quota Increases: If you are expanding your team and need additional visa capacity, supporting documentation including the translated MOA may be required.

Manager and Partner Visas: Shareholders and managers applying for residence visas under the company often need translated MOA documents showing their role and ownership stake.

Investor Due Diligence and Corporate Transactions

When your company is raising capital, entering a joint venture, or being acquired, counterparties conduct legal due diligence. Translated corporate documents are part of this process:

Investor Review: Potential investors want to understand your ownership structure, governance provisions, and any restrictions on share transfers. A professionally translated MOA demonstrates that your corporate house is in order.

Legal Opinions: UAE lawyers issuing legal opinions for transactions rely on Arabic versions of corporate documents. Having an accurate translation available streamlines this process.

Merger and Acquisition Due Diligence: In M&A transactions, buyers review the target company's constitutional documents. Translated MOAs are part of the document bundle that legal teams analyze.

MODULE E

Frequently Asked Questions

These are the questions we hear most often from business owners, PROs, and corporate lawyers dealing with MOA translation. If your situation is not addressed here, reach out and we will provide guidance specific to your case.

What is the difference between a Mainland MOA and a Freezone MOA?

A Mainland MOA is registered with the Department of Economic Development (DED) and follows UAE Commercial Companies Law. It includes specific articles about capital structure, partner obligations, profit distribution, and management that align with federal commercial law requirements.

A Freezone MOA is governed by the regulations of the specific free zone authority (DMCC, JAFZA, DIFC, etc.) and often follows a template prescribed by that authority. The provisions may differ from mainland requirements, particularly around foreign ownership, activity descriptions, and governance structures.

The translation approach differs because mainland MOAs require terminology that DED and UAE courts recognize, while freezone MOAs must align with the specific authority's expectations. We adapt our translation to match the regulatory context of your company.

Can I amend my MOA and have only the amendment translated?

Yes, we regularly translate MOA amendments, addendums, and supplementary agreements without requiring the entire MOA to be retranslated. This is common when you are changing partners, adjusting share capital, or adding new business activities.

However, we recommend providing the original MOA and any previous amendments for context, even if you only need the latest amendment translated. This ensures consistency in terminology, party names, and cross-references. If the amendment refers to "Article 7 of the Original MOA," for example, having that article helps us translate the amendment accurately.

How do you handle share structure and capital changes?

Share structure translation requires precision because banks, DED, and investors rely on these figures for ownership verification. We translate:

  • Total share capital and currency denomination
  • Number of shares issued and their par value
  • Percentage holdings for each shareholder
  • Any capital increase or decrease provisions
  • Pre-emption rights and share transfer restrictions

We verify that the translated figures match the source document exactly and that percentages sum correctly. For complex structures with multiple share classes or preference shares, we use established Arabic legal terminology and may include transliterated terms where appropriate to preserve clarity.

What happens when a partner changes or exits the company?

Partner changes typically require documentation that we can translate as a package:

  • Share Transfer Agreement: The agreement transferring shares from the exiting partner to the remaining partners or a new partner
  • Partner Exit/Entry Acknowledgment: Any acknowledgment or consent documents signed by the parties
  • Amended MOA: The updated MOA reflecting the new ownership structure
  • Board Resolution: Any resolution approving the transfer

For mainland companies, the complete set of translated documents will be needed for DED registration of the change. For freezone companies, each authority has its own procedures. We ensure the translated documents maintain consistency so that the ownership transition is clearly documented.

Is Notary Public attestation required for MOA translation?

The attestation requirements depend on where the MOA was executed and where you intend to use the translation:

UAE-Executed MOAs: If your MOA was signed before a UAE Notary Public, the notary seal typically provides sufficient authentication for domestic use. The translation does not require additional attestation.

Foreign-Executed MOAs: If the MOA was executed abroad (for example, for a branch of a foreign company), the standard attestation chain applies: notarization in the country of origin, foreign ministry authentication, UAE Embassy attestation, and MOFA attestation. Translation should happen after this chain is complete.

Bank Requirements: Some banks have their own attestation requirements for translated documents. We can advise based on which bank you are dealing with and their specific compliance procedures.

How long does MOA translation take for bank account opening?

For standard MOAs of 5-10 pages, we can typically complete translation within 24-48 hours. If you have a bank appointment scheduled, let us know the date and we will prioritize accordingly.

Banks often request multiple documents beyond the MOA — board resolutions, shareholder passports, trade license, proof of office address. If you send us the complete package, we can translate everything together and ensure consistency across documents. This is more efficient than translating documents one at a time and reduces the risk of terminology discrepancies.

For urgent account openings, same-day express translation is available. Contact us by WhatsApp to discuss your timeline.

Can you translate MOAs with complex corporate structures?

Yes, we regularly handle MOAs for holding structures, subsidiaries, joint ventures, and multi-tier ownership arrangements. These documents often include detailed provisions about:

  • Management authority and reserved matters
  • Dividend distribution policies
  • Inter-company transaction procedures
  • Exit mechanisms and tag-along/drag-along rights
  • Deadlock resolution procedures
  • Non-compete and confidentiality obligations

We translate these provisions carefully to preserve the legal relationships and obligations. For particularly complex structures, we may consult with legal specialists familiar with UAE corporate law to ensure terminology accuracy. We also maintain consistency if you need related documents — parent company MOA, subsidiary MOAs, shareholders' agreements — translated together.

If your situation does not fit neatly into any of the above, reach out with a brief description and your MOA document. We will review it and provide specific guidance within a few hours.

UNDERSTANDING POA TRANSLATION

Why Power of Attorney Translation Requires Extra Care

A Power of Attorney is not a standard contract or certificate. It is a delegation of your legal identity to another person. The stakes are higher because the document will be used to make binding decisions on your behalf — sometimes involving significant sums of money or irreversible transactions.

In the UAE, Arabic is the governing language for most official and judicial proceedings. This means that when you present a POA to Dubai Land Department, a bank, a Notary Public, or a free zone authority, the Arabic version is what they will rely upon. If there is any ambiguity or discrepancy between the English and Arabic texts, the Arabic text typically prevails. This creates a situation where even small translation choices can have significant consequences.

Consider a General POA that grants authority to "manage all financial affairs." In Arabic, there are multiple ways to express this concept, and the choice of words can expand or narrow the scope of authority. A translator unfamiliar with UAE legal practice might use a phrase that sounds correct but is interpreted more restrictively by a bank's legal department. The result: your agent is unable to complete the very transaction the POA was meant to authorize.

Similarly, a Special POA for property sale must reference the property correctly. DLD expects to see the Makani number, plot number, or other official identifiers that match their records. If the translation uses a different format or omits required details, the registration officer may refuse to process the transaction until corrections are made — adding days or weeks to your timeline.

We approach POA translation with these realities in mind. Before starting work, we review the document to understand its purpose and intended use. We ask questions about which authorities will receive it and whether there are any specific requirements you have been told to meet. This front-end diligence helps avoid back-end problems.

MODULE A

POA Types and Compliance Requirements

Before translating your Power of Attorney, it helps to understand the different types recognized in the UAE and the compliance requirements that apply to each. This knowledge allows you to confirm that your document is complete and properly executed before you invest in translation.

Types of Power of Attorney in the UAE

General Power of Attorney (Wakala 'Amma)

A General POA grants broad authority to the agent (attorney-in-fact) to act on the principal's behalf across multiple matters. This may include managing bank accounts, signing contracts, handling property transactions, representing the principal before government authorities, and conducting business operations. General POAs are commonly used by business owners who travel frequently or by individuals who need someone to manage their affairs during extended absences.

Special Power of Attorney (Wakala Khassa)

A Special POA limits the agent's authority to a specific transaction or set of transactions. For example, a Special POA might authorize an agent to sell a particular apartment, collect rent from a specific property, or open a single bank account. Because the scope is narrow and clearly defined, Special POAs are often preferred for property transactions at DLD, where clarity about what the agent can and cannot do reduces the risk of disputes.

Limited Power of Attorney

Similar to a Special POA but sometimes used to describe authority that is limited by time, transaction type, or monetary threshold. For example, a Limited POA might authorize an agent to manage a company's affairs for one year only, or to sign contracts up to a certain value without requiring additional approval.

Healthcare Power of Attorney

Less common in the UAE but occasionally needed for medical decisions when the principal is incapacitated or unavailable. Healthcare POAs authorize an agent to make medical treatment decisions, access medical records, or consent to procedures on the principal's behalf.

Notarization Requirements for POAs

POAs Executed in the UAE

If you are signing a POA in the UAE, it typically needs to be executed before a Notary Public. The Notary will verify your identity (usually through Emirates ID or passport), witness your signature, and apply their official seal. In Dubai, notary services are available at Dubai Courts and through authorized Notary Public offices. The notarized POA can then be translated into Arabic by an MOJ-licensed translator for use with banks, DLD, or other authorities.

POAs Executed Abroad

If the POA was signed outside the UAE, additional steps are usually required before it can be used here. The typical attestation chain includes: notarization in the country of origin, authentication by the foreign ministry or equivalent, attestation by the UAE Embassy in that country, and finally MOFA (Ministry of Foreign Affairs) attestation upon arrival in the UAE. Only after this chain is complete should the POA be translated. We can review your document and advise on whether attestation is complete or if additional steps are needed.

Apostille Convention Countries

For POAs from countries that are party to the Hague Apostille Convention, the process may be simplified. An Apostille certificate replaces the traditional embassy attestation chain. However, UAE authorities may still require MOFA attestation of the Apostille in some cases. Requirements evolve, and different authorities may have different expectations, so we recommend checking current practice for your specific situation.

Dubai Land Department (DLD) Requirements for Property POAs

DLD is one of the most common authorities where translated POAs are presented, and they have specific expectations:

  • Property Identification: The POA should clearly identify the property using its Makani number, plot number, building name, unit number, or other official identifiers that match DLD records.
  • Transaction Scope: The POA should explicitly state what the agent is authorized to do — purchase, sale, mortgage, lease registration, transfer, or other specific actions.
  • Parties: Full names and passport/Emirates ID numbers of the principal and agent should be clearly stated and consistent with identification documents.
  • Validity: While DLD does not always mandate a specific validity period, some registration officers prefer POAs that are relatively recent (within 6-12 months). If your POA is older, we can advise on whether a new POA might be prudent.
  • Arabic Translation: The translation must be certified by an MOJ-licensed legal translator, with the translator's stamp, signature, and license number visible on each page.
  • Attestation for Foreign POAs: If the POA was executed abroad, DLD typically requires the complete attestation chain (home country notarization, foreign ministry, UAE Embassy, MOFA) before they will accept it.

MODULE C

After-Care Guide: Using Your Translated POA

Translation is just one step in the process. The real value of a well-prepared POA translation is realized when you present it to the authority, bank, or counterparty that needs to rely upon it. This guide covers what to expect after you receive your translated POA and how to navigate common scenarios.

Using Your POA at Dubai Land Department (DLD)

For property transactions, you or your agent will present the translated POA at the DLD Trustee office or through the Dubai REST app for certain procedures. Here is what to expect:

The registration officer will verify that the POA covers the specific transaction you are attempting to complete. For a sale, they will check that the POA authorizes the agent to sell the identified property. They will compare the property details in the POA against their records and may ask for supporting documents such as the title deed or sale agreement.

If the POA was executed abroad, be prepared to show the complete attestation chain. Officers may request to see the original notarization, foreign ministry authentication, UAE Embassy attestation, and MOFA stamp. Having all these documents organized and readily available speeds up the process.

DLD may also verify the identity of the agent presenting the POA, typically through Emirates ID or passport. If the agent's name in the POA differs slightly from their ID (for example, a middle name omitted), this can sometimes cause delays. We recommend addressing any name discrepancies before the appointment if possible.

Bank Account Operations with POA

UAE banks are cautious when accepting POAs for account access or transactions. Each bank has its own internal compliance procedures, and what one bank accepts, another may question. Here are practical considerations:

Account Opening: Most banks are reluctant to open new accounts based solely on a POA. They typically prefer the account holder to be present for the initial account opening and KYC (Know Your Customer) procedures. A POA is more commonly used to operate an existing account.

Transaction Limits: Banks may impose limits on what an agent can do under a POA, even if the POA itself is broadly worded. For example, they might allow routine transactions but require additional verification for large transfers or account closures.

Registration of POA: Some banks require you to formally register the POA with them before the agent can use it. This involves submitting the original and translated POA to the bank's legal department for review, which can take several days. Plan accordingly if you have time-sensitive transactions.

Recent POAs: Banks often prefer POAs that are relatively recent — ideally issued within the last three to six months. If your POA is older, the bank may request a new one or additional confirmation from the principal.

DMCC and Freezone Company Management

If you own or manage a company in DMCC, JAFZA, DAFZA, or another free zone, POAs are commonly used to authorize managers, PROs, or legal representatives to act on the company's behalf. Each free zone has its own procedures:

DMCC: POAs for company management typically need to be attested and translated before the authority will accept them for portal transactions, visa applications, or license renewals. DMCC may require specific wording or formats, so we recommend checking their current guidelines or having us review your POA against their requirements.

JAFZA: Jebel Ali Free Zone Authority has its own forms and procedures for authorizing representatives. In some cases, they may accept a general corporate resolution rather than a POA. We can advise on which document type is appropriate for your situation.

Other Free Zones: Each free zone — DIFC, ADGM, Dubai South, Sharjah free zones, and others — has its own approach. If you are unsure what your free zone requires, send us the POA and tell us what you need to accomplish. We will advise on the translation requirements and any additional steps needed.

Court Proceedings and Litigation

If the POA is intended to authorize legal representation in court proceedings, additional considerations apply. The agent (typically a lawyer) must be authorized to appear before the specific court in question. For Dubai Courts, the POA should clearly state the lawyer's name and bar registration details. For DIFC Courts or ADGM Courts, different admission requirements apply.

The translated POA will be submitted as part of the court file, and the judge may review it to confirm the lawyer's authority to represent you. Any ambiguity in the POA's scope could be raised by the opposing party as a procedural challenge.

Keeping Records and Managing Validity

We recommend keeping copies of both the original and translated POA in a secure location. If the POA has an expiration date, note it in your calendar so you can arrange for renewal before it lapses. If circumstances change — for example, you sell the property or no longer need the agent to act for you — consider executing a formal revocation to limit any future misuse.

MODULE E

Frequently Asked Questions

These are the questions we hear most often from clients dealing with POA translation. If your situation is not addressed here, reach out and we will provide guidance specific to your case.

What is the difference between General and Special Power of Attorney?

A General Power of Attorney grants broad authority to the agent to act on your behalf across multiple matters — property, banking, legal affairs, and more. It is suitable when you need someone to handle your affairs comprehensively, such as during extended travel or if you live abroad and have ongoing business in the UAE.

A Special Power of Attorney limits the agent's authority to specific, defined transactions. For example, it might authorize your agent to sell a particular apartment but nothing else. Special POAs are preferred for property transactions at DLD because they clearly define what the agent can do, reducing the risk of unauthorized actions or disputes.

When translating either type, we ensure the Arabic text accurately reflects the scope of authority as stated in the original document. We may also advise if we notice a mismatch between the POA type and your intended use.

Can my Power of Attorney be used for property purchase or sale in Dubai?

Yes, a properly executed and translated POA can be used for property transactions at Dubai Land Department. However, DLD has specific requirements that must be met:

  • The POA must explicitly mention the property, ideally using the Makani number or other official identifiers that match DLD records.
  • The POA must state the nature of the transaction (purchase, sale, mortgage, lease registration, etc.).
  • If the POA was executed abroad, it must be fully attested through the appropriate chain (home country, UAE Embassy, MOFA).
  • The Arabic translation must be certified by an MOJ-licensed legal translator.

We format property POA translations to meet DLD expectations and can advise if your POA appears incomplete or unclear for its intended purpose.

Does my Power of Attorney need to be attested before translation?

It depends on where the POA was executed. If the POA was signed in the UAE before a Notary Public, it typically does not require additional attestation for domestic use. The notary seal itself provides the authentication needed.

If the POA was executed abroad, it generally needs authentication from your home country's authorities plus UAE Embassy attestation and MOFA attestation before it can be used in UAE government departments, banks, or DLD. The translation should happen after the attestation chain is complete, so all stamps and seals are captured in the translated document.

We can review your POA and advise on whether attestation appears complete or if additional steps are needed before translation.

Can I revoke a Power of Attorney after it has been translated?

Yes, a POA can generally be revoked at any time by the principal (the person who granted the power). The revocation must be communicated properly to be effective. In the UAE, this typically involves:

  • Executing a formal revocation document before a Notary Public
  • Having the revocation translated into Arabic by an MOJ-licensed translator
  • Notifying all relevant parties (banks, DLD, free zones, counterparties) of the revocation

We can assist with the translation of revocation documents and guide you on the notification process to ensure the revocation is properly recorded.

How long does a Power of Attorney remain valid in the UAE?

POA validity varies based on how the document was drafted. Some POAs specify an expiration date (for example, "valid for one year from the date of execution"), while others remain valid until formally revoked.

In practice, different authorities have different preferences. DLD registration officers may prefer POAs issued within the last 6-12 months. Banks often want POAs that are relatively recent (3-6 months). If your POA is older, you may be asked to provide a new one or confirm that the principal's intentions have not changed.

We recommend checking with the specific institution where you plan to use the POA and can advise based on your intended use case.

Can a Power of Attorney be translated if it is in a language other than English?

Yes, we handle POA translations from multiple source languages including French, German, Russian, Hindi, Urdu, Spanish, Portuguese, Chinese, and many others. The UAE's diverse population means we regularly encounter POAs executed in various countries and languages.

For some language pairs, translation may proceed through an intermediate step (for example, Russian to English to Arabic). We will explain the process, timeline, and any cost implications when you submit your document for review.

What documents do I need to provide along with my Power of Attorney?

At minimum, we need a clear scan or photo of the complete POA document including all pages, signatures, notary stamps, and any attestation stamps. For effective review and accurate translation, please ensure:

  • All corners of the document are visible
  • Text is legible and not blurred
  • Stamps and seals are clearly captured
  • Any annexes or schedules referenced in the POA are included

For property-related POAs, it helps to have a copy of the title deed or property details so we can verify consistency. For corporate POAs, the trade license or company registration may be useful context. We will review your submission and let you know if anything additional is needed before proceeding.

If your situation does not fit neatly into any of the above, reach out with a brief description and your POA document. We will review it and provide specific guidance within a few hours.

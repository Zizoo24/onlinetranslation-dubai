Understanding Death Certificate Translation Requirements in the UAE

When a family member passes away, the administrative requirements that follow can feel overwhelming. If the deceased held assets in the UAE, such as bank accounts, property, investments, or business interests, you will likely need an MOJ-certified Arabic translation of the death certificate to proceed with legal formalities. This is not optional; it is a requirement from banks, the Dubai Land Department, courts, and other government entities.

The translation must be prepared by a translator licensed by the UAE Ministry of Justice. This means the translated document carries an official stamp, a QR code for verification, and the translator's license number. Without this certification, the translation will not be accepted for legal proceedings or official transactions. We work with licensed translators who specialize in vital records and understand both the technical requirements and the sensitive nature of these documents.

Beyond the translation itself, the death certificate typically needs to go through an attestation chain before it becomes legally valid in the UAE. This involves getting the document attested by the relevant authority in your home country, followed by your country's Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the UAE Embassy in your home country, and finally UAE MOFA (Ministry of Foreign Affairs). The exact requirements can vary based on your nationality and the specific use case, which is why we provide guidance on the complete process rather than just the translation step.

Common Uses for Death Certificate Translation in Dubai

Death certificate translations are required for a wide range of legal and administrative purposes in the UAE. Each institution has its own procedures, but all require the same foundation: an MOJ-certified translation of the original death certificate. Understanding these use cases helps you prepare the right documentation from the start.

Bank Account Closure

UAE banks require a translated and attested death certificate to close accounts, transfer funds to beneficiaries, or release frozen assets. Each bank has specific documentation requirements, but the MOJ-certified translation is universal. The process typically involves multiple visits and additional documentation such as succession certificates or legal heir affidavits.

Property Transfer

The Dubai Land Department and equivalent authorities in other emirates require death certificate translation for transferring property ownership to heirs. This applies to both freehold properties and leasehold interests. The translation forms part of a larger package that includes inheritance documents, identity papers, and sometimes court orders.

DIFC Probate

For estates with DIFC connections or involving non-Muslim expatriates, the DIFC Courts Probate Registry provides an alternative to local Sharia courts. Proceedings require translated death certificates meeting specific formatting requirements. We have experience preparing documents for DIFC Court submissions.

Sharia Court Inheritance

For Muslim deceased or when beneficiaries prefer Sharia court proceedings, translated death certificates are essential for initiating inheritance distribution. The courts may also require marriage certificates, birth certificates of heirs, and other supporting documents, all needing certified translation.

Company Share Transfer

If the deceased held shares in a UAE company, transferring ownership to heirs requires documentation including the translated death certificate. This applies to mainland LLCs, free zone companies, and offshore entities. Each jurisdiction has different procedures, but all need the core translated documents.

Vehicle De-registration

De-registering vehicles owned by the deceased requires proving death and authority to act. While this is often handled as part of larger estate proceedings, standalone vehicle transfers may be necessary, particularly for leased vehicles or when selling rather than transferring to heirs.

What We Translate and Certify

Death certificates vary significantly depending on the country and even the specific jurisdiction of issue. Some are detailed multi-page documents, while others are simple single-page certificates. Regardless of format, we translate every element that appears on your original document, ensuring nothing is lost in translation.

We translate all identifying information including full legal name, date of birth, place of birth, nationality, passport or identification numbers, and last known address. Names are transliterated into Arabic following UAE conventions, and we maintain consistency if you have other documents (such as the deceased's passport or residency visa) that show established Arabic name spellings. This consistency is important because discrepancies between documents can cause delays with banks and government authorities.

The core death information includes date of death, time of death if recorded, place of death (hospital, residence, or other location), and cause of death. Some jurisdictions include detailed medical information while others simply state "natural causes" or a general category. We translate exactly what appears on your certificate. For certificates in medical terminology, our translators are familiar with standard medical Arabic equivalents used in UAE healthcare and legal contexts.

Many death certificates include information about the deceased's parents, spouse, and sometimes surviving family members. This information can be relevant for inheritance purposes, particularly in Sharia court proceedings where heir identification is central. We translate all family member names and relationships as they appear on the certificate, maintaining the same transliteration approach used for the deceased's own name.

Death certificates typically bear official stamps, registrar seals, and authorized signatures. We translate any text within these stamps (such as "Registrar of Deaths" or the name of the issuing office) and note the presence of seals and signatures in the translation. This helps UAE authorities understand the authenticity markers on your original document. Any attestation stamps added during the legalization process are also translated.

Official registration numbers, certificate numbers, and any reference codes that appear on the death certificate are included in the translation. These numbers may be needed when the UAE authority wants to verify the authenticity of the original certificate through official channels. We present these numbers clearly in both the original format and Arabic numerals where applicable.

Attestation Guidance

For most official uses in the UAE, simply translating the death certificate is not enough. The original document must also be attested through the proper diplomatic channels. This attestation chain proves to UAE authorities that the document is genuine and issued by a recognized authority in your home country.

The standard attestation process for death certificates follows this sequence:

Home Country Authority

Notarization or authentication by the relevant government department, such as the Department of Foreign Affairs, Secretary of State, or equivalent body depending on your country.

Home Country Foreign Ministry

The Ministry of Foreign Affairs in your home country attests the document, confirming the previous attestation is valid. For Apostille countries, this step may be combined or replaced by Apostille.

UAE Embassy

The UAE Embassy or Consulate in your home country attests the document, recognizing it for use in the UAE. Processing times vary by embassy.

UAE MOFA

The UAE Ministry of Foreign Affairs provides final attestation, after which the document is fully recognized by all UAE authorities. This is the final step before translation.

The question of whether to attest before or after translation depends on your specific situation. In most cases, attestation happens on the original document, and translation comes after. However, some scenarios require different approaches. We are happy to advise you on the correct sequence for your particular use case and home country combination.

Death Certificates from Different Countries

Dubai's expatriate population means we regularly encounter death certificates from countries around the world. Each country has its own format and attestation requirements. Our experience with this diversity helps us anticipate potential issues and guide you through the correct process.

Indian Death Certificates: Usually issued in English, sometimes with regional language elements. Common states include Maharashtra, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Delhi. Attestation typically goes through the state Home Department, MEA, and UAE Embassy. We see many Indian death certificates and are familiar with their standard formats.

Pakistani Death Certificates: Issued in Urdu and English. Attestation involves MOFA Pakistan and the UAE Embassy in Islamabad or the Consulate in Karachi. NADRA documentation may also be involved for identity verification purposes.

British Death Certificates: Issued by the General Register Office in England and Wales, NRS in Scotland, or GRONI in Northern Ireland. Apostille replaces embassy attestation for UK documents. We translate certificates from all UK jurisdictions.

American Death Certificates: Each US state issues its own format, and attestation requirements vary by state. Many documents can use Apostille, but some require additional Secretary of State certification. We have handled death certificates from nearly all US states.

Philippine Death Certificates: Issued by PSA (Philippine Statistics Authority) and require DFA authentication plus UAE Embassy attestation. We regularly translate Filipino death certificates and are familiar with PSA document formats.

Egyptian and Other Arab Country Certificates: Already in Arabic, these typically need only attestation and possibly translation of non-Arabic elements. We verify completeness and can provide certified copies formatted for UAE authority preferences.

If your death certificate is from a country not mentioned here, please reach out. We have experience with documents from over 120 countries and can advise on the specific process for your situation.
